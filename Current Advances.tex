\chapter{Current Advances} 
\sffamily
\label{chap:Literature_Review}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

This report is a representation of the progress that has been made by the Aerodynamic Test Platform and Wings team during the 2025-2026 academic year. As stated in the summary of this report, the breakdown of this teams structure does not follow one main result, rather it encompasses three. While each subsection within the team is focused on the analysis of flapping forces, the progress of each is discussed separately.

\indent For the Aerodynamic Test Platform (ATP), Stan and Kevin have taken this year to solidify its use as a tool for other teams within the MFWF project. The prototype that was received at the start of the year was required a redesign to initialize testing. Through the iterative testing process, the platform was modified in its structure and electronics to support the fine resolution required for capturing the small forces produced by the MFWF. One of these redesigns involved the removal of the tension cable method of attaching the inner frame to the load cells. The large amount of compensation needed to sustain it was concluded to be unnecessary for the ATP's purpose. A successful redesign involved the use of rigid links to replace the tension cables, resulting in consistent force measurement without the need for compensation, but resulted in higher visibility of the force coupling effect on the 6-axis design. The platform was validated through a series of tests using the flapping actuators available, confirming its ability to be used as a tool for measuring qualitative differences in lift production. In-depth advances can be found in sections \ref{chap:Stan_Melchers} and \ref{chap:Kevin_Fair}.

\indent For the ATP Neural Network, Wade and Aidan have taken on the newly assigned responsibilities to learn about and apply machine learning to the Aerodynamic Test Platform (ATP) of the MFWF. The fall semester involved the development of the a proof-of-concept aerodynamic mode to predict aerodynamic coefficients. Wade began with an Long Shot-Term Memory (LSTM) model and transition to a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) model. Aidan began and developed an MLP model during the fall semester. The scope of work shifted at the beginning of the winter semester to replicate a published academic paper that utilizes similar data to the anticipated unsteady aerodynamic data from the ATP and the Rotation Rates Test Stand (RRTS) to predict non-linear aerodynamic forces and moments. Wade replicated a Probabilistic Recurrent State-Space (PR-SSM) model and Aidan replicated a sequence-to-sequence model. For Wade and Aidan's in-depth advances, see sections \ref{chap:Wade_Schnurr} and \ref{chap:Aidan_Sheridan}, respectively.

\indent As for the MFWF's wings, Abdul and Justin took significant steps in designing, manufacturing, and dynamically screening a structured set of wings for the MFWF flyer. Abdul led the wing design, literature review, manufacturing process development, DOE execution, and design verification. A comprehensive review of published literature was conducted to define the deformation targets that govern lift generation in hovering flapping wing flight. A revised manufacturing process was developed and validated. The Wings and process was confirmed structurally viable. A provisional wing recommendation of R-V-C5.0 (ID:~F) was made based on mass compliance and qualitative camber formation observed during spin testing, pending quantitative ATP validation. Justin focused on developing a dynamic testing and motion tracking framework to evaluate wing deformation during flapping. T A marker-based tracking system was developed in MATLAB using HSV colour segmentation to help extract displacement data from testing. A structured testing environment was established, with ROI masking and span-axis filtering implemented to reduce false detections and improve marker identification. For in-depth advances by Abdul and Justin, see sections \ref{chap:Abdulrahman_Sirajudeen-Sanni} and \ref{chap:Justin_Denneny}, respectfully.
