\subsection{Background}

The Wing design teams over 2 years established structural, material, and testing knowledge that defined and set a baseline for the wing design efforts this year.  
In this section, summaries of key design and manufacturing contributions of prior teams, the limitations of their work, and how these gaps shaped the objectives pursued this year are discussed.

\subsubsection{2021/22 Team}

The 2021/22 team established a foundational wing architecture and manufacturing 
methodology for the Twist Modulation controlled wing. Their primary contribution was a hybrid four-component structural scheme consisting of a 3D-printed vein structure, a carbon fibre leading edge rod, a flexible 
membrane, and a root control bar, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:baselinewingmodified}. 

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=4.5cm]{figures/baselinewingmodified.png}
    \caption{Baseline Wings: Final Wing Design from Previous Capstone Year \cite{MFWF_FinalReport2021}}
    \label{fig:baselinewingmodified}
\end{figure}

This architecture aimed to balance stiffness during stroke reversal and compliance under root actuation, while remaining practical to manufacture iteratively. Manufacturing method and process for this architecture is detailed in \cite{MFWF_FinalReport2021}

Material selection was conducted in parallel. For the vein structure, four filaments 
were evaluated --- Standard PLA, PETG, ABS, and LW-PLA. ABS was eliminated due to 
print difficulty and high density. No significant stiffness difference was found 
between the remaining three, so LW-PLA was selected solely on a weight basis, 
achieving densities as low as 0.6~g/cm\textsuperscript{3} at a nozzle temperature of 
250\textdegree C. For the flexible membrane, RC covering film was eliminated early due to its heat-activated adhesive and inconsistent brand quality. Both polypropylene tape and Kapton tape were carried forward; polypropylene tape was 0.4~g lighter per wing but exhibited gradual plastic deformation during use, while Kapton offered higher tear resistance and dimensional stability at a weight penalty. Both were deemed viable pending dynamic testing. Five carbon fibre leading edge rod sizes were also analyzed Rods with an outer diameter of 1.5~mm and above produced acceptable tip deflections, while the 1.0~mm rod was found to be marginal at 8.64~mm.

\subsubsection{2022/23 Team}

The team's key finding in the 2022/23 year was that the thin vein regions near the wing root were found to be susceptible to cracking and fatigue under the inertial loading experienced at 30~Hz stroke 
reversal, identifying a clear need for local geometric reinforcement in subsequent 
designs. They addressed the root stiffness issue by increasing the diameter of the root control bar ~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2023}.

\subsubsection{Gaps Motivating This Year's Work}
Despite the progress made by both prior teams, the body of work remained confined to wings designs specifically suited for twist modulation. This year, a foundamental change in the control approach and decision to work with and electromagnetic flapping mechanism  forced a total redesign of the wing. The new frequency based control approach eliminated the need for twist modulation, which required a seperated root control bar and leading edge rod. In addition, the body of work remained confined to static characterization. Static tests are effective for screening structural behaviour under a fixed load, but do not capture the time-varying aerodynamic and inertial forces experienced during flapping. Key quantities directly linked to lift generation --- chordwise camber ratio, passive twist, and spanwise bending amplitude --- cannot be reliably measured in a static setting. Furthermore, while viable materials and geometries had been identified, no systematic framework existed to relate specific design parameters to these aeroelastically relevant deformation modes. Wing selection 
had therefore relied on static deflection comparisons and mass compliance alone.These gaps directly shaped this year's work.

Wing design and manufacturing tasks this year were initially focused on refinig and reproducing the wing architecture proposed during the previous years. Durability improvements were introduced at the root to address the cracking observed, and new vein geometries were designed and manufactured.  We then moved on to define ideal wing charateristics through extensive literature review. The verification focus also shifted to designing dynamic experiments and manufacturing test wings. The deflection and bending measuremeents were to be compared against target ranges established through a literature review of insect-scale flapping wing research correlated to lift generation. The wing was then redesigned to meet the interface requirements of the updated electromagnetic flapping mechanism, which also required the development of a new manufacturing process. And lastly, a structured Design of Experiments was developed to evaluate a 3 straight veined carbon fibre reinforced polypropolene wing wing with a built-in camber angle of 15 degrees. The wing requirements and physical constraints 
remain unchanged from prior years.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The wing must be able to provide sufficient lift to the flyer.
    \item The wing must provide a means of attitude control via root actuation.
    \item To ensure the flyer is controllable, the wings must deform in a uniform and predictable manner.
    \item The wings must meet the following physical constraints
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Each wing may have a span of up to 10 cm.
        \item Each wing can weigh up to 1.5 grams.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item The wing must be able to withstand the forces imparted on it for the duration of the flight. There are two peak loads of concern:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Peak inertial loads experienced during stroke reversal.
        \item Peak aerodynamic loads experienced at mid-stroke.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item The wings should be easy to manufacture and with consistent results.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Literature Review}

Building on prior MFWF work~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2021, MFWF_FinalReport2023}, a focused review of flapping-wing micro air vehicle (FWMAV) literature was conducted to identify deformation characteristics, structural parameters, and mass distributions that directly govern lift generation in hovering flight. Unlike fixed-wing aerodynamics, lift in FWMAVs is dominated by unsteady mechanisms, particularly leading-edge vortex (LEV) formation, wake capture, and rotational circulation~\cite{ellington1999, dickinson1999wing}.

To ensure quantitative relevance, this review incorporates experimentally validated data from both biological flyers (e.g., hawkmoths, fruit flies) and engineered platforms such as the Harvard RoboBee ~\cite{ma2013robobee}, DelFly Nimble ~\cite{de2018delfly}, and KUBeetle ~\cite{phan2020kubettle}. These systems provide well-documented benchmarks for structural stiffness, and aerodynamic performance as they have all flown successfully.

Across insect-scale flyers and engineered FWMAVs, three coupled deformation modes consistently appear: chordwise camber, spanwise bending, and passive twist. Figure \ref{fig:wingliftcharacteristics} shows the formation characteristics contributing to a wings ability to generate lift.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=6.5cm]{figures/wingliftcharacteristics.png}
    \caption{Wing Deformation Characteristics of a Flapping Wing \cite{dao2020corrugation}}
    \label{fig:wingliftcharacteristics}
\end{figure}

These deformation modes emerge from the interaction between aerodynamic loading, inertial forces, and structural compliance~\cite{shyy2010aerodynamics, ishihara2009fluid}. Importantly, these behaviors are strongly influenced by wing mass distribution and stiffness gradients, which define the dynamic response.


\subsubsection{Mass Scaling and Representative Flyers}

Wing and body mass directly constrain lift requirements and influence optimal deformation behavior. Reported values from literature include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fruit fly (Drosophila melanogaster):} body mass $\sim$1 mg, wing mass $\sim$0.02 mg (2\% of body mass)
    \item \textbf{Hawkmoth (Manduca sexta):} body mass $\sim$1.5--2.5 g, wing mass $\sim$50--100 mg
    \item \textbf{Harvard RoboBee:} total mass $\sim$80 mg, wing mass $\sim$3 mg per wing, flapping at $\sim$120 Hz~\cite{ma2013robobee}
    \item \textbf{DelFly Nimble:} total mass $\sim$29 g, wingspan 33 cm, wing mass $\sim$0.5--1 g~\cite{de2018delfly}
    \item \textbf{KUBeetle-S:} total mass $\sim$15 g, wing mass $\sim$0.2--0.4 g, flapping frequency $\sim$20 Hz~\cite{phan2020kubettle}
\end{itemize}

Across these systems, wing mass is typically 1--5\% of total body mass, with lower fractions observed in highly optimized flyers. Reduced wing mass lowers inertial power requirements and enables higher flapping frequencies, but must be balanced against structural stiffness to maintain controlled deformation. The mass constraint of 1.5g for the MFWF falls around 3.75\% in line with the findings. 

\subsubsection{Chordwise Camber}

Chordwise camber enhances lift by stabilizing the LEV during the downstroke and delaying flow separation. Experimental and numerical studies report optimal camber ratios in the range of $f = h/c = 5\%$--$12\%$ for Reynolds numbers between $10^2$ and $10^4$~\cite{wu2011aerodynamic, du2010aerodynamics}. Lift coefficient increases of 20--40\% relative to flat plates have been reported under comparable kinematics~\cite{luo2013effect}.

Camber is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    f = \frac{h}{c}
\end{equation}

In RoboBee wings (membrane thickness $\sim$5 $\mu$m), passive camber emerges due to aerodynamic pressure and inertial effects. Larger platforms such as DelFly employ thicker membranes ($\sim$10--25 $\mu$m), resulting in reduced camber but improved structural robustness. Camber in Wing designs for the MFWF should should thefore be targeted within 5--12\%, with attention to membrane thickness and tension.  

\subsubsection{Passive Twist}

Passive twist introduces spanwise variation in angle of attack, improving lift distribution and delaying tip stall. Reported twist angles range from $15^\circ$--$25^\circ$ in both biological flyers and engineered systems~\cite{yoon2014effect, kraemer2012rapid}.

In KUBeetle and DelFly designs, twist is achieved through graded stiffness, with torsional rigidity decreasing toward the wing tip. This allows phase lag between root motion and aerodynamic loading, producing beneficial twist without active control.

Lift improvements of 10--30\% compared to rigid wings have been measured~\cite{mountcastle2010aerodynamic}. However, excessive twist ($>30^\circ$) reduces effective angle of attack and lift generation.


Using a dynamic dfelection test twist for each design should be measured across multiple spanwise points. Mass distribution plays a key role here, a heavier tip will increase lag and can amplify twist beyond optimal ranges leading to loss of lift.

\subsubsection{Spanwise Bending}

Spanwise bending is driven primarily by inertial forces and aerodynamic loading. Typical deflections range from 10--20\% of the semi-span~\cite{combes2003flexural}. In hawkmoths, peak bending occurs near mid-span, while engineered wings often concentrate flexibility closer to the root.

Moderate flexibility improves aerodynamic efficiency by up to 15\% through load redistribution and reduced peak stresses~\cite{kang2011effects}.

\subsubsection{Material Properties and Structural Design} 

Wing deformation is strongly governed by the distribution of stiffness across the structure. A common design pattern observed in both biological and engineered wings includes: 
\begin{itemize} 
    \item Stiff leading edge (carbon fiber or reinforced vein) 
    \item Flexible membrane (low Young’s modulus, $\sim$1--10 MPa) \item Compliant trailing edge 
\end{itemize} 

Increasing leading-edge stiffness has been shown to reduce excessive bending while preserving aerodynamic shape. For example, increasing a carbon fiber rod diameter from 1.5 mm to 2.0 mm reduced wing deflection by approximately 40\% with minimal mass penalty in prior MFWF work~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2023}. 

Material selection directly affects repeatability and manufacturing variability. Literature highlights that small inconsistencies in membrane tension or vein placement can significantly alter deformation behavior, introducing experimental uncertainty. 

\subsubsection{Flapping Kinematics and Operating Conditions} 

Typical hovering FWMAVs operate within: 
\begin{itemize} 
    \item Frequency: 10--30 Hz 
    \item Reynolds number: $10^2$--$10^4$ 
    \item Stroke amplitude: $90^\circ$--$140^\circ$ 
\end{itemize} 
The Strouhal number ($St = fA/U$) is often maintained between 0.2 and 0.4 for efficient lift generation~\cite{taylor2003flying}. These parameters define the aerodynamic regime in which deformation characteristics must be evaluated. 

Dynamic testing on the ATP must replicate these operating conditions to ensure meaningful comparison with literature. Testing across multiple frequencies and amplitudes is also required to capture nonlinear behavior. 

\subsubsection{Experimental Considerations and Risks} 

Several practical limitations are consistently reported in literature: 
\begin{itemize} 
    \item \textbf{Measurement Noise:} Optical tracking systems introduce uncertainty of $\sim$1--3 pixels, requiring calibration and filtering~\cite{wang2005measurement} 
    \item \textbf{Cycle Variability:} Significant variation between flapping cycles necessitates averaging over multiple cycles 
    \item \textbf{Manufacturing Variability:} Small differences in membrane tension or bonding lead to inconsistent deformation \item \textbf{Structural Failure Modes:} Leading-edge buckling and membrane fatigue are common under high-frequency loading 
\end{itemize} 

These risks directly impact the reliability of ATP measurements and must be accounted for in experimental design, particularly in defining acceptable uncertainty and repeatability thresholds.

\subsection{Design of Experiment}




A structured Design of Experiments (DOE) Table was developed to identify which wing architectures reproduce the similar deformation ranges established in the literature review. The DOE is fully defined, the baseline wing was remanufactured, an initial prototype set has been fabricated, and dynamic testing will proceed once the dynamic testing  setup is ready in the winter term. The objective is to determine how variations in membrane material and vein geometry influence camber, twist, and spanwise bending during flapping, while all other parameters remain same as in the baseline wing.

Following standard DOE principles, only a small set of variables are being varied, and all other conditions are held constant. The wings share a fixed span determined using the max span constraints mentioned earlier to ensure consistent inertial loading, while chord size, chord curvature, membrane choice, and vein geometry were selected as the experimental factors. This approach isolates the structural contributions of each design parameter, ensuring that observed differences in deformation can be attributed to controlled changes.

The prototypes span three built-in geometric camber levels (low, medium, high), implemented as camber angle extension to the membrane cutout, see Figure \ref{fig:camberangle}. 

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=6.5cm]{figures/camberangle.png}
    \caption{Camber Angle Extension and Twist Angle in Wing Design\cite{yoon}}
    \label{fig:camberangle}
\end{figure}

Membranes include polypropylene (PP) tape and Kapton, with Mylar included if available due to its favorable stiffness-to-weight properties. Vein geometry variations were introduced to shape chordwise flexibility and twist characteristics, including changes in vein stiffness and trailing-edge compliance.

All prototypes will undergo a dynamic deflection-tracking test and High-speed imaging will quantify deformation over the wingbeat cycle, including camber ratio and tip deflection as a measure of spanwise bending.

\begin{equation}
    f(t) = \frac{h(t)}{c},
\end{equation}
passive twist
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\text{twist}}(t) = \theta_{\text{root}}(t) - \theta_{\text{tip}}(t),
\end{equation}

These quantities will be evaluated at mid-downstroke, stroke reversal, and mid-upstroke, allowing direct comparison to the ideal deformation targets reported for insect-scale wings.

The DOE provides a systematic and traceable framework for selecting the best-performing wing prototypes. Wings will be assessed on quantitative criteria: camber within $5$--$12\%$, twist between $15^\circ$ and $25^\circ$, spanwise bending within $10$--$20\%$ of the semi-span. A new wing variant with a modified leading-edge bar and control-bar geometry, introduced to meet the requirements of the electromagnetic flapping mechanism, will be evaluated under the same protocol. This experimental design ensures that the selected final wing is supported by measurable evidence and reflects the deformation behaviour of each wing prototype.

\subsection{Prototypes}
Prototyping this term focused on rebuilding a reliable set of baseline wings, and improving manufacturability. Since no testing has been conducted yet, all work centered on fabrication, refinement, and preparing the prototypes for testing. These tests will validate functionality of the prototypes

A major portion of the effort involved remanufacturing the baseline wing used in previous years. Early prints showed issues with vein thickness uniformity, and bubbles in the tape membrane. The baseline design was therefore modified to introduce curved edges, reprinted multiple times, with adjustments to print orientation, support strategy, and extrusion temperature. These changes resulted in improved prints.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=4.5cm]{figures/newlymanufacturedwing.png}
    \caption{Baseline Wing Remanufactured with PETG Veins}
    \label{fig:newlymanufacturedwing}
\end{figure}

Beyond reproducing past work, the baseline geometry was also modified to improve fatigue resistance. Thin regions near the root and trailing edge experienced cracking during handling, indicating insufficient durability for prolonged dynamic motion. Local sharp internal corners were smoothed, and vein intersections were thickened slightly to distribute cyclic loads more evenly. These small geometric changes preserve the aeroelastic behaviour of the original design while significantly increasing the wing’s operational lifespan.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=4.5cm]{figures/baselinedesignchanges.png}
    \caption{Design Changes to Improve the Baseline Wing}

A substantial amount of time went into resolving 3D-printing issues. LW-PLA required careful tuning to avoid voiding, layer delamination, and uncontrolled expansion. Several prints failed due to nozzle clogging, inconsistent extrusion, and instability when printing thin trailing edge veins for some prototypes. Adjustments included slowing down perimeter speeds, and increasing bed adhesion using custom brims. Printer calibration also had to be redone to correct some of these issues.

Once the baseline was manufactured successfully, attention shifted towards designing new wings that span the design variations outlined in the DOE. Multiple prototypes were fabricated with different chord sizes, built-in camber levels (low, medium, high), membrane materials (PP and Kapton), and vein geometries, see Figure \ref{fig:prototypes}. The 3D printed veins shown in Figure \ref{fig:prototypes} were not fully manufactured at the end of the term due to uncertainty in the interface between the the control bar and our test flapper, as the team is unsure what mechanism will be ready in time for the dynamic wing test.

The final prototyping task involved modifying the leading-edge bar and control-bar design. This change was required after the decision to remove twist modulation by the electromagnetic flapping-mechanism team. Their system demands a different interface that can deliver the same stiffness near the root. The updated control bar and leading edge rod design improves load transfer into the wing, and ensures compatibility with the actuator’s stroke pattern. This modified bar will be used for all wings intended for dynamic testing next term. A more detailed version made of carbon fibre will be fabricated once interface decisions have been agreed on my the two teams during the winter term.

\subsection{Plan for Winter Semester}
Work in the winter semester will focus on analysing the results from dynamic deflection-tracking tests and comparing the measured wing deformation to the ranges reported in the literature. From the high-speed recordings, quantitative metrics such as instantaneous camber ratio, root-to-tip twist, and spanwise bending amplitude will be extracted across multiple points in the flapping cycle. Additional timing data, such as phase lag between the leading edge and trailing edge, will help identify whether a wing responds too stiffly or too softly under dynamic loading. These measurements will directly inform the next round of prototypes by identifying which vein geometries or membrane materials produce desirable deformation patterns and which designs deviate from expected behaviour. Wings that demonstrate controlled camber formation, smooth passive twist, and repeatable bending profiles will be refined and re-printed for aerodynamic lift testing. 
In parallel, remaining interface questions with the electromagnetic flapping-mechanism team will be finalized to ensure that the revised leading-edge bar and control-bar designs integrate cleanly with their actuation system. Together, this work will establish a clear deformation-based selection pathway for the prototypes proceeding to aerodynamic evaluation.
