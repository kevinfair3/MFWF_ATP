
\subsection{Background}

The wing design teams over the previous two years established the structural, 
material, and testing knowledge that defined the baseline for this year's 
wing design efforts. This section summarises key contributions of prior teams, 
the limitations of their work, and how these gaps shaped this year's objectives.

\subsubsection{2021/22 Capstone Contributions}

The 2021/22 team established a foundational wing architecture and manufacturing 
methodology for the twist modulation controlled wing. Their primary contribution 
was a hybrid four-component structural scheme consisting of a 3D printed vein 
structure, a carbon fibre leading edge rod, a flexible membrane, and a root 
control bar, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:baselinewingmodified}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=4.5cm]{figures/baselinewingmodified.png}
    \caption{Baseline wing: final wing design from the 2021/22 capstone 
    year~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2021}.}
    \label{fig:baselinewingmodified}
\end{figure}

Material selection was conducted in parallel. For the vein structure, four 
filaments were evaluated: Standard PLA, PETG, ABS, and LW-PLA. ABS was 
eliminated due to print difficulty and high density. LW-PLA was selected 
over the remaining options on a weight basis, achieving a printed density 
of 0.6~g/cm\textsuperscript{3} at a nozzle temperature of 250\textdegree C, 
the lowest of the three. For the flexible membrane, RC covering film was 
eliminated early due to its heat-activated adhesive and inconsistent 
availability. Both polypropylene tape and Kapton tape were evaluated; 
polypropylene tape was 0.4~g lighter per wing but showed plastic deformation 
during handling, while Kapton offered higher tear resistance at a weight 
penalty. Five carbon fibre leading edge rod diameters were analysed; rods 
of 1.5~mm outer diameter and above produced acceptable tip deflections under 
the predicted inertial loading at 30~Hz, while the 1.0~mm rod produced a 
marginal tip deflection of 8.64~mm~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2021}.

\subsubsection{2022/23 Capstone Contributions}

The 2022/23 team identified that thin vein regions near the wing root were 
susceptible to cracking under the cyclic inertial loading at 30~Hz stroke 
reversal. They addressed this by increasing the leading edge carbon fibre 
rod diameter to 2~mm, with a marginal weight penalty~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2023}.

\subsubsection{Gaps Motivating This Year's Work}

Both prior teams worked exclusively within a twist modulation control 
framework and confined their characterisation to static loading conditions. 
Static tests are effective for screening structural behaviour under a fixed 
load but do not capture the time-varying aerodynamic and inertial forces 
experienced during flapping. Key quantities directly linked to lift 
generation, specifically chordwise camber ratio, passive twist, and spanwise 
bending amplitude, cannot be reliably measured in a static setting. No 
systematic framework existed to relate specific design parameters to these 
aeroelastically relevant deformation modes.

This year, the electromagnetic flapping mechanism team removed twist 
modulation from the control architecture, eliminating the aerodynamic 
requirement for a separated root control bar and leading edge rod 
arrangement. This change required a total wing redesign compatible with 
the updated mechanism interface and a frequency-based control approach. 
Wing design and manufacturing tasks consequently progressed from refining 
the prior architecture, through a full structural redesign, to developing 
a new manufacturing process and a structured Design of Experiments targeting 
dynamic deformation performance. The wing requirements and physical 
constraints remain unchanged from prior years and are listed below.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The wing must provide sufficient lift to the flyer.
    \item The wing must provide a means of attitude control via root actuation.
    \item The wings must deform in a uniform and predictable manner.
    \item The wings must meet the following physical constraints:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Each wing may have a span of up to 100~mm.
        \item Each wing may weigh up to 1.5~g.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item The wing must withstand peak inertial loads at stroke reversal 
    and peak aerodynamic loads at mid-stroke.
    \item The wings must be easy to manufacture with consistent results.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, the team attempted to meet all six requirements but fell short 
in several. The testing, results, limitations, and recommendations sections 
detail the lessons learned and define a path forward.

\subsection{Literature Review}
\label{sec:lit_review}

Building on prior MFWF work~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2021, 
MFWF_FinalReport2023}, a focused review of flapping-wing micro air vehicle 
(FWMAV) literature was conducted to identify deformation characteristics, 
structural parameters, and mass distributions that directly govern lift 
generation in hovering flight. Unlike fixed-wing aerodynamics, lift in 
FWMAVs is dominated by unsteady mechanisms, particularly leading-edge 
vortex (LEV) formation, wake capture, and rotational 
circulation~\cite{ellington1999, dickinson1999wing}.

To ensure quantitative relevance, this review incorporates experimentally 
validated data from both biological flyers and engineered platforms 
including the Harvard RoboBee~\cite{ma2013robobee}, DelFly 
Nimble~\cite{de2018delfly}, and KUBeetle~\cite{phan2020kubettle}, all of 
which have demonstrated sustained hovering flight and provide well-documented 
benchmarks for structural stiffness and aerodynamic performance.

Across insect-scale flyers and engineered FWMAVs, three coupled deformation 
modes consistently appear: chordwise camber, spanwise bending, and passive 
twist, as shown in Figures~\ref{fig:wingliftcharacteristics} 
and~\ref{fig:Wing deformations}.

These deformation modes emerge from the interaction between aerodynamic

\subsubsection{Mass Scaling and Representative Flyers}

Wing and body mass directly constrain lift requirements and influence 
optimal deformation behaviour. Reported values from literature include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fruit fly (\textit{Drosophila melanogaster}):} body 
    mass $\sim$1~mg, wing mass $\sim$2\% of body mass~\cite{ellington1999}
    \item \textbf{Hawkmoth (\textit{Manduca sexta}):} body mass 
    $\sim$1.5--2.5~g, wing mass $\sim$50--100~mg per wing
    \item \textbf{Harvard RoboBee:} total mass $\sim$80~mg, wing mass 
    $\sim$3~mg per wing, flapping at $\sim$120~Hz~\cite{ma2013robobee}
    \item \textbf{DelFly Nimble:} total mass $\sim$29~g, wing mass 
    $\sim$0.5--1~g per wing~\cite{de2018delfly}
    \item \textbf{KUBeetle-S:} total mass $\sim$15~g, wing mass 
    $\sim$0.2--0.4~g per wing, flapping at $\sim$20~Hz~\cite{phan2020kubettle}
\end{itemize}

Across these systems, wing mass is typically 1--5\% of total body mass. 
The MFWF vehicle has a total mass of 40~g, placing the 1.5~g per wing 
constraint at 3.75\% of vehicle mass, which is consistent with the upper 
end of this range. Reduced wing mass lowers inertial power requirements 
and enables higher flapping frequencies, but must be balanced against 
structural stiffness to maintain controlled deformation. The operating 
Reynolds number for the MFWF wing at 30~Hz with a 5.0~cm chord and 
100~mm span is approximately $Re = \bar{U}c/\nu \approx 3{,}000$--$5{,}000$, 
placing it firmly within the unsteady low-Reynolds-number regime 
($10^2$--$10^4$) where LEV-dominated lift mechanisms apply.

\subsubsection{Chordwise Camber}

Chordwise camber enhances lift by stabilising the LEV during the downstroke 
and delaying flow separation. Experimental and numerical studies report 
optimal camber ratios of $f = h/c = 5\%$--$12\%$ for Reynolds numbers 
between $10^2$ and $10^4$~\cite{wu2011aerodynamic, du2010aerodynamics}, 
where $h$ is the maximum camber height measured perpendicular to the chord 
line and $c$ is the local chord length. Lift coefficient increases of 
20--40\% relative to flat plates have been reported under comparable 
kinematics~\cite{luo2013effect}. Camber is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    f = \frac{h}{c}
\end{equation}

In RoboBee wings (membrane thickness $\sim$5~$\mu$m), passive camber 
emerges from aerodynamic pressure and inertial effects. Larger platforms 
such as DelFly employ thicker membranes ($\sim$10--25~$\mu$m), producing 
reduced camber but improved structural robustness. Camber in the MFWF 
wing should therefore be targeted within 5--12\%, with attention to 
membrane thickness and its effect on passive deformation magnitude.

\subsubsection{Passive Twist}

Passive twist introduces spanwise variation in angle of attack, improving 
lift distribution and delaying tip stall. Reported twist angles range from 
$15^\circ$--$25^\circ$ in both biological flyers and engineered 
systems~\cite{yoon2014effect, kraemer2012rapid}. It is noted that 
biological twist arises from anisotropic material properties not present 
in carbon fibre and polypropylene constructions; the applicability of these 
target ranges to the MFWF wing is therefore an assumption that ATP testing 
is intended to validate rather than confirm. In KUBeetle and DelFly designs, 
twist is achieved through graded stiffness, with torsional rigidity 
decreasing toward the tip, producing beneficial phase lag between root 
motion and aerodynamic loading without active control. Lift improvements 
of 10--30\% compared to rigid wings have been measured~\cite{mountcastle2010aerodynamic}, 
though excessive twist ($>30^\circ$) reduces effective angle of attack 
and lift.

\subsubsection{Spanwise Bending}

Spanwise bending is driven primarily by inertial forces and aerodynamic 
loading. Typical deflections range from 10--20\% of the semi-span in 
biological and engineered flyers~\cite{combes2003flexural}. These values 
are reported from quasi-static and low-frequency measurements; dynamic 
amplification at 30~Hz may shift the effective range, and this must be 
accounted for when interpreting ATP measurements against these targets. 
Moderate flexibility improves aerodynamic efficiency by up to 15\% through 
load redistribution and reduced peak stresses~\cite{kang2011effects}.

\subsubsection{Material Properties and Structural Design}

Wing deformation is governed by the distribution of stiffness across the 
structure. A consistent design pattern in both biological and engineered 
wings includes a stiff leading edge (carbon fibre or reinforced vein), a 
flexible membrane (Young's modulus $\sim$1--10~MPa), and a compliant 
trailing edge. Increasing leading-edge stiffness reduces excessive bending 
while preserving aerodynamic shape; increasing a carbon fibre rod from 
1.5~mm to 2.0~mm diameter reduced deflection by approximately 40\% with 
minimal mass penalty in prior MFWF work~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2023}. 
Small inconsistencies in membrane tension or vein placement can 
significantly alter deformation behaviour, making manufacturing 
repeatability a direct experimental quality concern.

\subsubsection{Geometry and Aspect Ratio}

Wings with an aspect ratio between 2 and 3 tend to achieve optimal 
aerodynamic efficiency~\cite{bhfly2025, oppenheimer2017}. A camber angle 
of $\delta = 15^\circ$ was identified as optimal by the BH-Fly programme; 
beyond this value, corrugation of the wing membrane during rotation was 
observed, degrading aerodynamic efficiency. This value was adopted as the 
built-in camber angle for the MFWF DOE wings. The area moment of inertia 
of the wing planform about the root is given by Equation~\ref{eq:area_moment}:

\begin{equation}
    I_A = \int_0^r y^2\, c(y)\, \mathrm{d}y
    \label{eq:area_moment}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $y$ is the spanwise coordinate and $c(y)$ is the local 
chord length~\cite{oppenheimer2017}. For a uniform rectangular planform, 
Equation~\ref{eq:area_moment} reduces to:

\begin{equation}
    I_A = \frac{r^3 c}{3}
    \label{eq:area_moment_rect}
\end{equation}

\noindent Substituting a fixed aspect ratio $\text{AR} = r/c$ into 
Equation~\ref{eq:area_moment_rect} yields:

\begin{equation}
    I_A = \frac{\text{AR}^3\, c^4}{3}
    \label{eq:area_moment_ar}
\end{equation}

\noindent showing that $I_A$ scales as the fourth power of chord for a 
fixed aspect ratio. The required $I_A$ for hover can be computed from:

\begin{equation}
    I_A = \frac{2mg}{\rho\, C_L(\alpha)\, A^2\, \omega^2}
    \label{eq:hover_condition}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $m$ is the vehicle mass, $g$ is gravitational 
acceleration, $\rho$ is air density, $C_L(\alpha)$ is the lift coefficient 
at angle of attack $\alpha$, $A$ is the stroke amplitude, and $\omega$ 
is the flapping frequency~\cite{oppenheimer2017}. This model assumes 
quasi-steady aerodynamics, which is a known simplification at low Reynolds 
numbers where unsteady effects dominate; it is used here for sizing 
guidance only.

\subsubsection{Flapping Kinematics and Operating Conditions}

Typical hovering FWMAVs operate within a frequency range of 10--30~Hz, 
Reynolds numbers of $10^2$--$10^4$, and stroke amplitudes of 
$90^\circ$--$140^\circ$. The Strouhal number ($St = fA/U$) is typically 
maintained between 0.2 and 0.4 for efficient lift 
generation~\cite{taylor2003flying}. Dynamic testing on the ATP must 
replicate these conditions to ensure meaningful comparison with literature, 
and testing across multiple frequencies and amplitudes is required to 
capture nonlinear behaviour.

\subsection{Wing Design Specifications}

The wing design presented here represents a departure from the prior MFWF 
baseline. The previous design relied on a stiff membrane with a fixed root 
attachment, where twist formation was partially achieved through membrane 
material properties. The updated design was developed to satisfy the 
revised interface requirements of the electromagnetic flapping mechanism, 
which removed twist modulation from the control architecture. Rather than 
relying on membrane stiffness, the new design allows the membrane to rotate 
passively around two carbon fibre control bars. Passive camber and twist 
are generated entirely through aeroelastic deformation under flapping loads, 
with the vein layout governing the deformation shape. Ease of assembly and 
the ability to mass-produce identical pairs were also core design objectives 
given the number of configurations required by the DOE.

\subsubsection{Wing Components}

The wing assembly consists of three primary structural components as 
illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:wingCAD}.

\paragraph{Control Bar:}
The control bar consists of two 1.5~mm diameter carbon fibre rods joined 
by a PETG 3D printed T-connector (100\% infill). It serves as the primary 
structural member through which the flapping mechanism transmits motion 
to the wing. The membrane is wrapped around the control bar to form a 
sleeve, allowing it to rotate freely about the bar axis. This sleeve 
attachment decouples membrane stiffness from the root boundary condition, 
enabling the reduced-stiffness design approach, as illustrated in 
Figure~\ref{fig:wingsleeve}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=6.5cm]{figures/wingsleeve.png}
    \caption{Sleeve specification adopted from~\cite{yoon2014effect}.}
    \label{fig:wingsleeve}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Wing Veins:}
The intermediate vein structure consists of straight 1.0~mm diameter 
pultruded carbon fibre micro rods bonded to the membrane surface using 
polypropylene tape. The veins run from the root toward the tip, and their 
angular placement relative to the chordwise axis defines the stiffness 
gradient across the wing. The angular arrangement, referred to as the 
vein splay pattern, is one of the two primary variables in the DOE. The 
specific angular values for each pattern (U, V, W) are documented in the 
DOE design matrix.

\paragraph{Flexible Membrane:}
The flexible membrane forms the aerodynamic surface. Polypropylene card 
sleeve material (PP5) was selected for its adequate tear resistance under 
dynamic flapping loads, confirmed through tear testing, and for its lower 
areal density relative to Kapton. The membrane is cut to the target 
planform shape using 3D printed templates to ensure dimensional 
repeatability across replicates. (Insert Template photos)

\subsection{Design of Experiments}

The DOE was developed to systematically identify which wing structural 
parameters produce deformation characteristics consistent with the 
literature-based targets in Table~\ref{tab:lit_summary}. Two Taguchi L9 
orthogonal array experiments were designed, one for each planform series. 
Within each series, two factors are varied: vein splay pattern (U, V, W) 
and chord length (4.5, 5.0, 5.5~cm), each at three levels, giving nine 
unique veined designs per series. An unmodified baseline wing is included 
in each series as a reference, bringing the total to ten designs per 
series and twenty unique designs overall. The two series are analysed 
independently and then compared to determine whether planform shape 
produces a systematic difference in deformation and lift response.

\subsubsection{Analysis Plan}

Results are analysed in two stages. In Phase~1, within each series 
independently, the mean response at each level of vein pattern and chord 
is computed and plotted as Taguchi main effect charts to identify which 
combination produces deformation and lift responses closest to the targets 
in Table~\ref{tab:lit_summary}. The unmodified baseline wing in each 
series (ID:~A and ID:~K) provides the reference against which the effect 
of adding the vein structure is quantified. In Phase~2, the 
best-performing wing from each series are compared directly, and matched 
pairs sharing the same vein pattern and chord across series (for example 
\texttt{R-V-C5.0} and \texttt{Z-V-C5.0}) are compared to isolate the 
effect of planform shape while holding vein geometry constant. The wing 
producing the best combination of deformation quality and lift output 
proceeds to aerodynamic refinement.

\subsubsection{Winter Semester: Revised Process, DOE Design, and 
Production}

\paragraph{Manufacturing Process Revision}
Early winter testing revealed that the inherited assembly process produced 
wings with excessive root stiffness. Two sources were identified: 
unintended membrane pre-tension introduced by the attachment method, and 
additional stiffness near the root from the rod-to-vein bonding. The 
revised process addresses both and consists of three stages: rod 
connection, membrane cutting, and vein and sleeve attachment.

\paragraph{Step 1: Rod Connection}
The two carbon fibre rods are cut to size and inserted into the PETG 
T-connector. If the fit is loose, the joint is bonded with hot glue or epoxy. Hot 
glue was used during early prototyping and as the standard for all DOE wings.

\paragraph{Step 2: Membrane Cutting}
Dedicated 3D printed templates were designed for each planform and chord 
size. The template is placed over the polypropylene membrane and the 
planform boundary is cut, ensuring all replicates of the same design are 
cut from identical shapes and eliminating planform variation as a source 
of replicate-to-replicate mass and stiffness scatter. (Insert Template photos)

\paragraph{Step 3: Vein Attachment and Sleeve Addition}
Vein rods are bonded to the membrane surface using polypropylene tape and 
positioned at the angular locations defined in the DOE design matrix. Angular placement is guided by lines 
drawn on the membrane for each vein angle. It is acknowledged that this 
method does not provide a verified angular accuracy; angular placement 
error is the primary unquantified source of manufacturing variability 
in the current process and is identified as the highest priority 
manufacturing improvement for future teams. A polypropylene sleeve is 
added at the root where the control bar passes through the membrane, 
bonded with tissue adhesive, allowing the membrane to rotate freely 
around the bar and significantly reducing root stiffness relative to 
the prior rigid bonded attachment. A summary of the manufacturing steps are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:manufacturing}

\paragraph{Quality Control:}
Each assembled wing pair is weighed on a precision scale. Wing pairs 
exceeding 3.00~g (the maximum allowable assembled pair mass, corresponding 
to 1.5~g per wing, exceptions were made for DOE wings) are rejected and remanufactured. Each wing is also 
visually inspected for membrane bubbles, vein misalignment, incomplete 
bonds, rod protrusion, and sleeve integrity before acceptance into the 
test pool.

\subsection{Design Verification and Results}

The full ATP testing was not completed this year due to mechanism
limitations detailed in the Limitations section. As a result, no 
quantitative lift or deformation data are available. A drill spin test 
was conducted as an improvised alternative to evaluate structural integrity 
and provide qualitative deformation observations. Manufacturing phase mass 
data, collected during quality control, provide the second source of 
quantitative results and are reported first.

\subsubsection{Manufacturing Phase Results}

Mass measurements across the DOE design space confirmed three independent 
effects on assembled wing mass.

\paragraph{Chord Length:}
Mass increases linearly with chord in both series, with the 1.5~g 
per wing constraint satisfied at or below 5.0~cm. All 5.5~cm 
configurations are retained for comparison but are ineligible for 
final selection.

\paragraph{Planform Series:}
Series~Z is consistently heavier than Series~R across all equivalent 
configurations due to the larger membrane area of the trapezoidal 
planform. Series~R is therefore preferred on mass grounds unless 
aerodynamic testing demonstrates a performance advantage for Series~Z 
that offsets the penalty.

\paragraph{Vein Pattern:}
Pattern~W produces the highest mass overhead in both series. Pattern~V 
is the most mass-consistent across chord lengths. Pattern~U at 4.5~cm 
in Series~R recorded the single largest overhead at $+22.7\%$ above 
baseline and is flagged for elimination.

\subsubsection{Drill Spin Test}

The drill spin test is a preliminary dynamic screening test in which 
wings are mounted in matched pairs on the T-bar and driven at 
progressively increasing speeds using a DeWalt 20V cordless drill, as 
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:drillspin}. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=8cm]{figures/drillspin.png}
    \caption{Drill Spin Test Set-Up.}
    \label{fig:drillspin}
\end{figure} 

The drill was operated at full 
speed on the high gear setting, corresponding to approximately 
1{,}800--2{,}000~RPM, equivalent to a flapping frequency of 30--33~Hz 
($f = \text{RPM}/60$), meeting or exceeding the 30~Hz target operating 
condition. Each wing pair was tested in both the clockwise and 
counter-clockwise directions to simulate the forward and return strokes 
of the flapping cycle and to ensure asymmetric failure modes were not 
missed. The test does not replicate the stroke reversal loading profile 
of the MFWF and cannot be used to quantify aerodynamic or deformation 
performance. Its purpose is limited to confirming structural integrity 
under dynamic loading and providing a first qualitative observation of 
membrane deformation behaviour. Results guide which configurations are 
carried forward but are not used as design selection criteria.

All 40 wings survived the drill spin test without catastrophic structural 
failure. No rod detachment was observed at the T-connector joints, 
confirming that the PETG connector and hot glue bond are adequate for the 
inertial loads at the tested drive speeds. The polypropylene sleeve 
functioned as intended in all wings, allowing free membrane rotation 
without tearing or delamination. No permanent leading edge rod 
deformation was observed, consistent with the 2021/22 structural 
analysis that identified 1.5~mm rods as acceptable at 
30~Hz~\cite{MFWF_FinalReport2021}. Qualitative deformation observations 
are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:results_summary}.

Based on the combined mass screening and drill spin test results, the 
optimal wing configuration identified from the available evidence is 
\texttt{R-V-C5.0} (ID:~F). Pattern~V in Series~R consistently produced 
the strongest qualitative camber formation in both the clockwise and 
counter-clockwise spin runs, indicating that the progressive vein 
arrangement promotes passive membrane deformation under dynamic loading. 
\texttt{R-V-C5.0} sits at the 3.10~g pair mass threshold, passed 
structural inspection with no joint failure, membrane tearing, or sleeve 
separation, and is the lightest mass-compliant Pattern~V configuration 
in Series~R. The secondary candidate is \texttt{Z-V-C5.0} (ID:~P), which 
also recorded strong camber, passed structural inspection, and sits at 
3.10~g, enabling a direct planform comparison against 
\texttt{R-V-C5.0}. This recommendation is provisional. It is based solely 
on mass compliance and qualitative spin test observation, not on the 
quantitative deformation and lift data the DOE was designed to generate. 
Final selection requires ATP testing with dynamic deformation tracking.

\subsubsection{Mitigating Actions Taken}
Despite the inability to complete the ATP test, the following actions 
preserve the value of this year's work for future teams.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Drill spin testing:} All 40 wings were tested, 
    confirming structural integrity and providing qualitative deformation 
    observations across the full design space.
    \item \textbf{Complete wing database:} All 40 wings are documented 
    with full design specifications, manufacturing parameters, mass 
    measurements, and spin test observations, allowing the next team to 
    execute the DOE without repeating fabrication work.
    \item \textbf{Manufacturing validation:} The revised process achieved 
    a replicate mass deviation of less than 2\%. All wings are in a 
    ready-to-test condition.
    \item \textbf{Experiment Manual:} The full 180-run test 
    protocol, measurement procedures, and analysis approach are 
    documented in this report and in the project handoff materials.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Recommendations for Future Work}
\label{sec:recommendations}

The three main recommendations for the next team are: securing a reliable 
flapping mechanism, completing ATP testing 
on the existing wing set or an expanded design set (Time constraints led to the reduced number of wings chosen for analysis), and improving manufacturing repeatability to 
consistently achieve the 2\% replicate deviation target with verified 
vein angular placement.

\paragraph{Secure a Reliable Flapping Mechanism:}
A mechanism producing consistent, controlled cycles up to 30~Hz with a 
quick-release wing interface is integral to efficient testing. The following requirements should ideally be met before any DOE wing is committed to testing.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Instantaneous flapping frequency must not vary within a cycle. 
    Cycle-to-cycle variation renders force measurements non-comparable 
    across wings and invalidates the DOE analysis.
    \item Peak-to-peak stroke amplitude must be recorded for every run 
    to enable normalisation.
    \item Easy Integration snd deintegration of the wing pair to the flapper.
    \item The mechanism should be validated on the two baseline wings 
    (\texttt{R-BASELINE}, ID:~A and \texttt{Z-BASELINE}, ID:~K) at 
    30~Hz across at least 10 consecutive runs before DOE wings are tested.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Complete ATP Testing on the Existing Wing Set or an Expanded Set:}
All 40 wings are manufactured and ready to test. If mechanism time is 
limited, the recommended testing priority order is: baselines first 
(ID:~A, K), then \texttt{R-V-C5.0} (ID:~F) and \texttt{Z-V-C5.0} 
(ID:~P), then remaining Pattern~V wings at 4.5~cm chord, then Pattern~U 
and W at 5.0~cm, and 5.5~cm chord wings last given their mass constraint 
failures.

\paragraph{Improve Manufacturing Repeatability:}
The current vein angular placement method, drawing lines on the membrane 
by hand, does not produce a verified angular accuracy and is the most 
significant uncontrolled variable in the DOE. Spending the first few 
weeks of the next project year developing and validating a more precise 
placement method, such as printed angular guides or direct FDM printing 
of veins onto the membrane, before remanufacturing the DOE wings is 
strongly recommended. This directly improves the quality and 
interpretability of any results.

\paragraph{Membrane Material Exploration:}
Polypropylene card sleeves were selected as an engineering compromise. 
Three alternatives are recommended in priority order.

\paragraph{Mylar (Biaxially Oriented PET Film):}
Mylar offers higher stiffness (2.5--5.0~GPa vs.\ 1.1--1.6~GPa for 
polypropylene) at lower density than Kapton and is available in 
thicknesses as low as 12~$\mu$m. Early trials this year confirmed 
physical compatibility with the current assembly process. A tensioning 
jig to achieve wrinkle-free application is required before Mylar wings 
enter the DOE. Test on the optimal geometry first before introducing 
as a full DOE variable.

\paragraph{0.51 oz Dyneema® Composite Fabric:}
This Ripstop nylon offers exceptional tear resistance and higher compliance 
than Mylar, making it well suited to wings where passive deformation is 
driven by membrane compliance. It could not be procured within this 
year's timeline and should be sourced from the RipstopByTheRoll website.

\paragraph{Direct 3D Printing onto Membrane:}
Printing the vein structure directly onto the membrane eliminates the 
bond line variability that is currently the most significant source of 
stiffness inconsistency between replicates. The primary challenge is 
adhesion to low-surface-energy polypropylene and Mylar surfaces.
\paragraph{Define the Actuator-Wing Interface as early as possible:}
Interface uncertainty was a direct contributor to this year's limitation on testing. At the start of next year, the wing and mechanism teams should 
jointly define the attachment interface specification in writing, produce 
a physical prototype and design for quick-release and 
compatibility with both available mechanism types from the outset. A 
wing pair swap time target of well under 3 minutes would be ideal. In addition, revisiting the option of a seperated root bar and leading edge rod is worthwhile is the main mechanism nest year is the mechanical version.
