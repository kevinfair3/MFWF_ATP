\subsection{Background}
The previous year decided to create a new design of the Aerodynamics Test Platform (ATP) from scratch. They preformed various computer simulations including Finite Element Analysis (FEA) for manufactured components and testing of various designs within MSC Adams to determine which would be the most stable under the oscillations induced by the flapper. Below is an image of the final ATP design CAD model.

  \caption{Previous Year's ATP Design.}
  \label{fig:Previous_ATP_Design}

The design consists of an outer frame made from aluminum extrusion and plastic press fit connectors. The base of the load cells are secured to the outer frame, the active end of the load cells are connected to crossbars using barrel adjusters and eight inch steel cables. There are six load cells within the design, two in each axis of motion. The crossbars are connected to the inner frame using more barrel adjusters and eight inch steel cables.

The previous year's team completed this design and was able to assemble the outer frame and some of the crossbars and cables. However, due to shipping timelines they were unable to assemble the entire design and begin testing.

In their handover letter, the previous year's team recommended the design of the inner frame be re-evaluated to better integrate with the flapper as well as the steel wire attachment mechanism between the inner frame and crossbars.

The semester began by reviewing the previous year's progress on the ATP, and getting a look at the ATP in person. Below is an image of the state of the ATP at the start of the year.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[height=6.5cm]{figures/Stan Melchers Figures/State of ATP Sep 2025.jpg}
  \caption{State of the ATP at the beginning of the Fall Semester.}

Looking at Figure \ref{fig:State_Of_ATP_Sep}, we see the aluminum outer frame, 3D printed inner frame and crossbars, as well as the steel cables connecting some of the components together. During inspections of the ATP it was observed that many of the 3D printed components were prototype components and were printed in multiple pieces and glued together afterwards, creating large variations in accuracy and build quality. Additionally, it was observed that the barrel adjusters did not offer much adjustment for the tensioning of the steel cables, being able to tension these cables would be necessary once the time came to integrate the load cells into the ATP. Based off the recommendations of the previous year's team and personal observations it was decided a re-design of the inner frame and attachment mechanism was necessary to increase the stiffness, resolution, and ease of assembly of the ATP.

\subsection{Creating a Functional ATP}
The goal for the fall semester was to create a functional ATP design that could be used with either the mechanical flapping mechanism or the electrodynamic flapping mechanism. To achieve this goal, we started with a redesign of the ATP's inner frame and coupling mechanism. After a couple weeks of design review and iteration, the inner frame design we settled on was two concentric 3D printed rings with an additional 3D printed adapter plate to which a flapper could be connected. The coupling mechanism was chosen to remain as the eighth inch steel cables with stud couplings due to time constraints as we wanted to get the ATP to a functional point as soon as possible. To connect to the load cells, stud anchors were used, the steel cables looped around the anchors and the anchors we're threaded into the load cells with the appropriate washers and nuts. With assembly complete, testing could begin. Testing was a joint effort with Kevin Fair as he is responsible for the Sensor Integration and Data Acquisition for the ATP. By this point in the semester, Kevin had gotten a single load cell functional with a laptop and LabVIEW to read the output voltage. We tested the load cell in both the horizontal and vertical orientations as well as various tensions for the steel cables. Our testing consisted of placing a series of decreasing masses onto the adapter plate and checking the output voltage of the load cell to determine the smallest detectable mass. During out initial tests, the smallest mass we detected was 200 grams. At a target flapper weight of 40 grams, this result was displeasing. Since this design utilizes steel cables, which work under tension, to achieve the desired rigidity these steel cables must be pre-tensioned. Our testing found that as this pre-tension force (And therefore the rigidity of the steel cables.) is increased, the resolution of our load cell decreased and vice-versa. Kevin talks in depth about this within section \ref{chap:Kevin_Fair} of this report. With the completion of this testing, that concluded our work on the ATP for the fall semester.

\subsection{Redesigning the Coupling Mechanism}
Starting the winter semester, the next step was to reduce the static mass of the inner frame and coupler design. With a lower static mass, the tensile forces required to support the design would be lower and therefore the resolution of the load cells would be increased. Since the inner frame was already 3D printed and therefore quite light, concentration was put on the steel cable coupling mechanism as this accounted for the major of the mass.

I began by creating a coupler design that utilized 3D printed threaded couplers and the eighth inch carbon graphite rod on hand from the wings team to connect the load cells to the inner frame. Figure \ref{fig:Rod Coupling Mechanism} shows a cross section of this design.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width = 10cm]{figures/Stan Melchers Figures/Rod Coupling Mechanism.png}
  \caption{Rod Coupling Mechanism.}
  \label{fig:Rod Coupling Mechanism}
\end{figure}

This design was sent for manufacturing, assembled, and tested once all components had been received. The testing revealed that the 3D printed couplers were too brittle and cracked at the base while being tightened to clamp onto the carbon graphite rod. With iteration it is possible that this design could have become a functional coupling mechanism design. However, at the same time as this testing, a second coupling mechanism was being designed and the potential for this second design seemed much higher. So, the decision was made to focus on the second design instead of revising the first design.

This second coupling mechanism design was a rigid design that utilized 3D printed square beams that would have heat set inserts pressed into them. One end of the beam would connect to the load cell using an threaded stud while the other end would connect to the inner frame using a Socket Head Cap Screw (SHCS) and washer. Figure \ref{fig:Rigid Coupling Mechanism} shows a cross section of this design.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width = 10cm]{figures/Stan Melchers Figures/Rigid Coupling Mechanism.png}
  \caption{Rigid Coupling Mechanism.}
  \label{fig:Rigid Coupling Mechanism}
\end{figure}

This design proved to be quite an improvement on the previous steel coupler design, the total mass of all the steel couplers was approximately 370 grams, while the total mass of the rigid coupling mechanism is approximately 120 grams. This greatly decreased the static mass on the load cells which meant we could increase the resolution of the load cells. This improvement was confirmed when we tested our design with the electrodynamic flapping mechanism. At both 8Hz and 10Hz the ATP was able to pickup 8 and 10 peaks respectively on the load cells. Additionally, the smallest static mass we could now detect was 10 grams, a great improvement from the 200 grams detectable with the steel couplers. Kevin was further able to improve this, but the mechanical design of the couplers remained the same for the remainder of the year. Figure \ref{fig:Assembled ATP Feb 2026} shows the ATP with the rigid couplings and the electrodynamic flapping mechanism during a testing session.

\subsection{Flapper Attachment Mechanisms}
With the design of the coupling mechanism complete, attention was then put towards testing the ATP. We had three methods of dynamically testing the platform:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The electrodynamic flapping mechanism
  \item A purchased flapper
  \item The mechanical flapping mechanism
\end{enumerate}
The electrodynamic flapping mechanism was first integrated to the platform because that was the mechanism that was progress most rapidly. The electrodynamic team had created their own 3D printed free standing design to hold their mechanism, integrating this to the platform was quite simple and just required adaptation to meet the adapter plate dimensions. Both the single and dual wing designs were created as see in figure \ref{fig:Electrodynamic Attachment Mechanism}. The flapping mechanism is held in place by a friction fit and the base is connected to the inner frame adapter plate using two SHCS and washers. These designs allowed us to test and compare the performance of both single and dual wing electrodynamic flapping at various frequencies.

A second notable effort was the manufacturing of a set of calibration weights which would be used to calibrate the load cells. Once it came time for Kevin to convert the load cell voltage readings into force readings, calibration weights were necessary to determine which voltage reading corresponded to which voltage. For our load cells it was determined that 1kg, 2kg, 3kg weights would be sufficient. After some iteration and discussion with the machinists, it was determined that the simplest way to create these weights was to cut lengths of 2" mild steel square stock and drill and tap a hole in one end so a threaded stud could be used to connect the weight to the load cell. This was done three times, once for each weight, leaving us with three weights slightly below our target masses. These masses did not have to be exactly 1kg, 2kg, and 3kg, these values were simply chosen as ballparks. With these weights manufactured, Kevin could then calibrate the load cells. \subsection{Redesigning the Outer Frame}
In the closing weeks of the semester, there was leftover funding for our capstone, so it was suggested by our lead engineers that the outer frame could be redesigned to use 25mm square aluminum extrusion instead of the existing aluminum square tube. The advantages of aluminum extrusion versus the existing design is primarily dimensional accuracy, the extrusion will produce an outer frame that is more accuracy and square than the existing frame improving the alignment of the load cells and their readings. Additionally, aluminum extrusion is very flexible in the accessories offered, this allows for future improvements to the frame to be quite simple in comparison to the current design. Another recommendation made by our lead engineers was the integration of vibrational dampers to mitigate the vibrations seen in the load cells during operation. 

With these recommendations in mind, the outer frame was redesigned accordingly. The outer frame decreased in size slightly for increased flexibility under operation and storage, the individual pieces of extrusion are held together using 90$^\circ$ angle brackets with bolts and nuts that fit into the slots of the extrusion. The load cells connect to the frame in the same method as the previous design, a clearance hole and corresponding SHCS and nut. The vibrational dampers mentioned earlier connect the load cell to the inner frame using a threaded stud on the load cell side and SHCS and washer on the inner frame side, similar to the rigid bar coupling mechanism. The inner frame remains unchanged from the previous design. Figure \ref{fig:Final ATP Design} shows an isometric view of the final CAD design of the ATP as well as a cross section.
