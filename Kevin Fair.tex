\subsection{Background}
The Aerodynamic Test Stand (ATP) is a platform used to measure the force of a centralized flapper in 6 separate directions. It achieves this by using SM S-type load cells to produce a millivolt (mV) differential signal proportional to the axial force seen by the load cells. This differential signal is amplified and converted to a voltage with reference to ground. The data acquisition unit (DAQ) is used to measure this voltage and LabView is used to display/record the values.

The 2022/2023 capstone year left the ATP electronics in pieces with a general construction schematic. The parts included five 50N SM S-type load cells, AD8556 amplifiers, the NI USB-6008 DAQ (borrowed), and two 7.4V batteries. Figure \ref{fig:PrelimSchematic} outlines how these parts were indented to fit together as constructed by the previous capstone year. Their goal laid out for this year was to manufacture the outlined architecture and achieve decoupled force measurement with the load cells while secured to the ATP. It should be stated that the 6th load cell was never present for testing this capstone year due to order and shipping delays, however it is acquired and ready for testing in the next capstone year.

\subsubsection{2025-2026 ATP Goals}
With knowledge of what the ATP electronics were meant to achieve and how the MFWF is going to flap, the overarching goal for this year was to manufacture an ATP that was able to accurately measure the orthogonal forces produced by a flapping wing. From a required weight of 40 grams \cite{MFWF_FinalReport2023}, the MFWF must produce at least the same amount of thrust to fly. This marked the general requirement of the resolution of the load cells. However, it was theorized that to accurately measure forces within the transverse directions (non-vertical), the ATP must measure forces much smaller, down to the 5-gram regime, to meet the requirement of accurately measuring all orthogonal forces.

Throughout the year, this was revised due to the lift forces that were being generated from both the electro-magnetic (EM) and mechanical actuators. With a lower thrust output that originally anticipated, the requirement of the ATP to measure within a 5-gram regime was extended to the dynamic lift measurement capability such that it could serve as a qualitative analysis for both these sub-teams and for the wing designers.

\subsubsection{Method Of Analysis}
As will become apparent within this report, measured values of force are not perfectly static. The signal measured changes with time and contains noise which can interfere with results. In terms of representing the acquired signal, the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is a common metric used when determining the quality of an expected signal compared to the measured one. While useful, one of the main goals of analyzing signals throughout this year was to determine how small of an offset could be detected over the noise, rather than how well a noisy signal could follow an expected one. This type of analysis was required when determining the resolution of the load cells and in determining the lift force generated by the flapper. For this reason, and due to the lack of tabulated data in the initial stages of testing, the SNR metric was not used during this year.

Instead, metrics for the offset identification type of analysis were used. Common methods are the limit of detection (LOD) and the limit of quantification (LOQ) where LOD represents the smallest offset that is ‘detectible’ and LOQ represents the smallest offset that is ‘accurately measurable’ \cite{LODLOQ}. Due to the nature of uncertainty, and the lack of trust in the early design stages of the ATP, the LOD method was used as proof that a signal could be seen above the noise and as a metric for expressing how small of an applied force the load cells could resolve statically. The expressions for both parameters are given as the following:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:LOD}
    LOD = \frac{3\sigma}{S}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation} 
    \label{eq:LOQ}
    LOQ = \frac{10\sigma}{S}
\end{equation}

where $S$ is the sensitivity of the the applied load in V/gram and $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of the noise in volts. To estimate $\sigma$ from a visual dataset, the highest conceivable value and lowest conceivable value can be used in the following \cite{SigmaEstimation}:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma = \frac{HCV - LCV}{6}
\end{equation}

It should be stated that this method of analysis was used to estimate the load cell resolutions from experimental data. It is not to be mistaken for absolute resolutions of the manufactured products, nor does it claim to be.

\subsection{Preliminary Testing}
After constructing the load string from Figure \ref{fig:PrelimSchematic} with only one load cell and amplifier, the first test performed was a general initialization test. The purpose was to validate the architecture previously conceived, however results of supplying power to the amplifier yielded no measurable force output. The AD8556 IC requires an input voltage of 5V and cannot amplify differential input signals that are close to its supply voltage rails \cite{Analog_AD8556_Datasheet}. The voltage of these differential inputs determined by the supplied voltage to the load cells. At 10V, this resulted in a common-mode voltage of 5V, which was within the amplifier's cut-off region. The resolution to this issue was to lower the supplied voltage to the load cells to 5V such that the common-mode voltage was 2.5V. The impact of this was a smaller range of voltage that could represent the capacity (0-50N) of the load cell . This range is termed 'sensitivity' in mV/V and is a characteristic of the load cell \cite{Interface_SM_SType}. Ideally, the highest voltage possible is used when powering the load cell to achieve a large range size and thus a higher resolution of force per voltage within the 15V supply limit of the SM S-type load cells.

Regardless of this deficiency, the 5V supply allowed the amplifier to output a measurable single-ended signal. To test the maximum resolution of the signal, a 50-gram weight was applied to the load cell, and the amplifier was set to its maximum gain of 1280 \cite{Analog_AD8556_Datasheet} in attempts to visually see a large voltage change as a result of the applied load. Figure \ref{fig:Prelim1280} shows the signal recorded of the unloading and reloading of this weight after a few attempts were made. From the graph, the measured signal is undoubtedly noisy, having an amplitude of about 0.09 V (shown in green) and the change in weight was measured as a 0.15 V offset (shown in red). 
\begin{figure}[htb]

Using the LOD metric described in the background section of this report, as seen in equation \ref{eq:LOD}, the lowest detectible force (weight) can be determined:
\[LOD = \frac{3\sigma}{S} = \frac{3 \cdot 0.09V}{0.15V/50g} = 30g\]
While this mertric passes the initial goal of measuring the MFWF’s lift force, it does not meet the requirement for measuring down to the 5-gram regime. Regardless, the same load string was tested while fastened to the ATP to validate its LOD of 30 grams while in-frame. The load cell was secured to the top part of outer frame and connected to the inner frame from its live end. This initial ATP design used steel cables as the connection method as seen in Figure \ref{fig:Assembled_ATP_E}.

The result of this type of connection is a large amount of tension to achieve any sort of rigidity within the frame. Rigidity is required to allow forces to pass through the cable without loss. With the small forces the ATP is required to measure, any sort of mechanical deflection would result in forces not being measured by the load cells.

One downside to this design was that the tension induced force on the load cells, which generated a large differential voltage signal going into the amplifiers. At a gain of 1280, the output was too high to be read by the DAQ due to its 10V limit. This required a reduction in the max gain applied to the amplifiers, from 1280 to 500. The second downside was the difficulty recreating the exact tension across tests. Each tension value required an alteration of the gain setting such that the amplifiers output did not exceed the 10V limit. The implication of operating at a lower gain was a smaller multiplication of the already small forces, ultimately lowering the resolution, or LOD, that could be achieved. To prove this, Figure \ref{fig:ATP_TensionTests} shows the ATP's response to a 50- and 200-gram weight applied. The purpose of these figures is to show the ATP's measurement with the same applied weight as in the preliminary unit test, and to show the smallest applied weight that could be visually detected with this ATP design.

\subsection{Electrical Improvements}
The main issue that was addressed from the preliminary testing was the amplifier limitations. The targeted features were a higher supply voltage to not limit the load cells excitation voltage, and a higher gain to achieve better resolution per load string. The amplifier chosen was the AD8421 due to its low noise, easily configurable gain, 32V input ceiling, and common use in load string applications \cite{Analog_AD8421_Datasheet}. Additionally, the NI-6008 DAQ was upgraded for the NI-6002 DAQ. This was a recommendation from the previous capstone year due to its ADC step size. The 6008 has a 12-bit range \cite{NI_USB6008_Specs} while the 6002 had a 16-bit range \cite{NI_USB6002_Specs}, essentially allowing a finer number of steps to be represented in the same measured range. With a $\pm$ 10V measured range, the smallest voltage each ADC could measure can be calculated as \cite{ADC_voltage}:
\[\text{NI-}6008 = \frac{20V}{2^{12}} = 4.88 mV\]
\[\text{NI-}6002 = \frac{20V}{2^{16}} = 0.305 mV\]
With the NI-6002 DAQ, a small measurement signal is able to be quantified with a finer resolution that the NI-6008 DAQ. This is especially important when trying to measure small forces with a small voltage change. 

A test was conducted to validate these improvements to the load string. One load cell was subjected to a 50-gram weight, similar to what was performed in the preliminary test for consistency. The variables changed were the supplied voltage to the load cell from 5 to 10 volts, and the applied gain from 1280 to 2000. The recorded unloading and reloading can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:NewELECUnitTest}. 
\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=4.5cm]{figures/NewDAQUnitTest.png}
    \caption{50g unload and load using upgraded electronics}
    \label{fig:NewELECUnitTest}  
\end{figure}
The main result of this figure is the significant reduction in noise compared to the signal offset due to the applied force. Using the LOD to quantify this, a minimum force of roughly 6.6 grams can be detected using this design. Knowing this is still a relatively unprecise metric, the result is clearly an improvement over the preliminary testing results of 30 grams, and much closer to the requirement of the 5-gram regime.

\subsection{Subtracting the Tension}
Regardless of the off-frame improvements to the resolution, the ATP’s flaw of a required tension on the load cells still limited the gain that could be used during testing. This limited the smallest force that could be measured. The tension was a purely static offset within the measured signal, and static offsets can be removed by subtracting the same (or similar) signals. This idea was realized by using another amplifier to subtract a known voltage from the load cell's signal. Figure \ref{fig:SubStringSchem} shows the block diagram of how this system was implemented. A small gain was used for the first amplifier such that the signal was just over 5V. A known 5V source was then subtracted from that signal and the difference was multiplied by the second stage gain. 
\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=6.5cm]{figures/SubStringSchem.png}
    \caption{Subtraction load string architecture schematic}
    \label{fig:SubStringSchem}  
\end{figure}

The benefit of this architecture is the application of a second stage gain that doesn’t get limited by the amplifier output or DAQ input because it should just be the small force measurement that gets amplified. The low-noise requirement of measuring such fine forces was realized by using clean power sources. A battery was used to avoid noise related to AC conversion, and linear drop-out (LDO) regulators were used to avoid switch noise found in DC-DC converters. This architecture also utilized a 12V load cell supply voltage such that the mapped voltage range for the load cell's capacity was as high as possible. 

To validate this design, multiple tests were performed with the subtraction load string connected to only the top load cell. Four static loads were placed onto the ATP, and the resultant voltage was measured from the DAQ. The loads were decremented until 10 grams to determine the best LOD. \subsection{Dynamic Testing}
Testing with static weights was not representative of the ATP’s intended use. While it did allow quantitative results in terms of force resolution, the more conclusive test was to measure force from an actuated wing motion. To first prove that the ATP could measure any force produced by an actuated wing, all five load cells were installed on the frame, and the EM actuator was placed in the center. The goal was to prove that the ATP could record forces at the frequency of the flapping mechanism, regardless of the precision in quantifying force. Figure \ref{fig:10HzFlapping} shows a frame of a recorded test where the EM actuator was operating at 10Hz.

    \caption{EM actuator at 10Hz signal response from ATP}
    \label{fig:10HzFlapping}  
\end{figure}
This frame is 1 second wide, and visually there are 10 peaks within it that represent the force produced by the flapping wing as it completes its flapping path. While not an accurate representation of the force produced each flapping cycle, this test was a confirmation that forces from the EM actuator were being measured by the ATP. Similarly, Figure \ref{fig:8HzFlapping} shows a frame of an 8Hz test with the EM actuator, and the flapping motion is also clearly measured by the ATP, showing 8 major peaks within one frame. 
\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=6.5cm]{figures/8HzFlapping.png}
    \caption{EM actuator at 8Hz signal response from ATP}
    \label{fig:8HzFlapping}  
\end{figure}
However, larger force and resonant signals are also measured within this test, whereas these features aren’t present within the 10Hz test. The cause of this was thought to be from the ATP’s structure vibrating as a result of the actuation, resonating with the flapping frequency. There was no real implication of this to the rest of the testing performed, as the design of the ATP went through a design iteration after this.

A large problem with the subtraction load string that became apparent as dynamic testing continued was the constant adjustment required. Potentiometers were used to control the gain set for each amplifier, and while useful, had inherent drift over time and resulted in a drifting signal from the first stage amplifier. The reason regular resistors were not used was due to constant change in tension experienced by the load cells and lack of methods to achieve perfect re-tension. Potentiometers allowed to account for this within the amplifiers but inherently had drift. Too large of a drift and the second stage amplifier was amplifying a large signal difference, and too small risked a “negative” subtraction output (where the load signal would be below the reference). 

The implications of both these cases were reduced force resolutions due to saturation. Similar to the AD8556, the AD8421 had issues outputting voltage close to certain rails, however unlike the AD8556, this was an internal restriction and not due to the power supply. The AD8421 is a two stage op-amp IC that first applies the gain to the differential input, then subtracts the signals. This flaw means the first op-amp can saturate when the common-mode voltage and gain applied are too large, changing the output from a linear relationship to a non-linear one. For an applied force, the voltage representation within the non-linear region will change less with higher applied force, thus a smaller resolution. Additionally, the extra components used for each load string added noise to the measured signal and, still without averaging and low-pass filtering, any reduction in noise was preferred.

To overcome these issues, a design change that was made that substituted the connector cables with rigid links, such that minimal tension was needed during operation. The electrical effect of this change can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:FinalStringSchem}, where the block diagram shows the updated architecture. 
\begin{figure}[htb]

    \label{fig:FinalStringSchem}  
\end{figure}
The string is similar to the one from the start of the year, but with some additional voltage regulation. The goal of this design was to reduce the number of interactions with the load signal to minimize noise. The smaller operation tension also allowed for only one gain stage to be used for high resolution. To verify this, static ATP tests were conducted with the same procedure as previously carried out, however only the 20- and 10-gram tests are compared in Figures \ref{fig:ATP_20Compare} and \ref{fig:ATP_10Compare} to illustrate the significant improvement in static force resolution.

\subsection{ATP Evaluation}
Returning to dynamic testing of the ATP, the only significant result so far was the measurement of forces at the same frequency as the flapper. The recorded signal contained high frequency noise and ATP vibrations when flapping. It is difficult to rely on the measured signal to be an accurate representation of the precise forces being produced from the flapping mechanism. In an ideal scenario, the force produced by each flapping stroke could be measured by the ATP accurately and in all 6 directions. In reality, this is very difficult given the Capstone’s budget and resources. Knowing the current ATP’s limitations in terms of dynamic signal resolution, a decision was made to utilize the design as an averaged force measurement instrument rather than trying to achieve the ideal case. This would allow qualitative analysis of a tested mechanism such that decisions could be made based on differences in force output in 6 directions.

To prove the ATP’s capability for this purpose, a series of tests were conducted to a) prove the accuracy of an averaged force measurement, b) prove the repeatability of an averaged force measurement, and c) prove the qualitative conclusions based on an averaged force measurement. This averaged force measurement was achieved by using a 4th order Butterworth filter and a rolling average filter, both within the LabView software. The implementation of this was a result of a few weeks of learning the LabView program and practicing with simulated signal manipulation when available.

\subsubsection{Accuracy}
To ideally prove the accuracy of averaged force measurement, a known dynamic force actuator was required. While concepts involving rotating eccentric masses and voice coil actuation were explored briefly, the simplest solution was to revisit the static loading. In determining lift force from an actuator, the most important loading was within the vertical axis. As a result, a static, 500-gram weight was applied where the flapper was attached within the frame and different supporting links were connected to the inner frame sequentially while the vertical force was being measured. The methodology behind this test was the result of validating the calibration done on the ATP. It was noticed that the vertical load cell placed at the top of the ATP frame measured a significantly lower force than if placed on the bottom of the frame. To quantify this inaccuracy, Figure \ref{fig:APT_NumbSchem} shows a schematic of the ATP with labelled links and Table \ref{tab:ATP_ForceTable} shows the force measurement from the load string based on the load cell position (top vs bottom) and the links connected to the inner frame (123456).

The results of this test show that the most accurate configuration was with the load cell placed at the top and no additional links connected to the frame, measuring 4.9N which is exactly 500 grams. As the 4 lateral links are connected to the frame, however, the measured force decreases, indicating a level of load sharing between the other links. The introduction of link 2 allows for an additional vertical static point, transferring more of the force to the top load cell. When the load cell is placed at the bottom of the frame, it is under compression. With no other links connected, the output voltage gets pushed into the lower saturation region of the amplifier, resulting in a non-linear force representation, as seen by $Bottom-2$. When link 1 is attached, it introduces tension on the load cell and the baseline is within the linear region, allowing for an 'accurate' measurement of the applied compression, as represented by the -4.36N data point. However, the addition of the other links again reduces the measured force to -3.41N, further indicating load sharing between the links.

To conclude this analysis, the static-linked ATP design is not accurate at measuring vertical averaged/static force applied within any of its fully connected configurations. This is due to the load sharing that is experienced as additional links connect to the inner frame. The most accurate measurement occurs when the vertical load cell is placed at the bottom of the frame, reading 3.41N when a 4.9N force is applied vertically. Given this 30\% error, the ATP can still be used for relative quantitative analysis for averaged force measurement, but any sort of accurate quantitative analysis would require a better design.

\[CV = \frac{\sigma}{\mu} \cdot 100\]
where $\sigma$ is the standard deviation and $\mu$ is the average of the collected sample. While CV is not standardized in terms of its “acceptable ranges”, generally 5\% is considered acceptable depending on the application \cite{Elsevier_CV}. To conclude this analysis, the ATP measurement of averaged force in the left and right load cells is acceptably repeatable given the maximum CV determined from a 6-iteration test of the flapping bird was 5.72\%. This is acceptable given the qualitative nature of the ATP with it's current design.

\subsubsection{Qualitative Analysis}
To prove the ATP can qualitatively discern the force output from different tests, variables for each developmental actuator were adjusted such that the measured force shows a qualitative difference. The distinction in qualitative compared to quantitative is used here due to the prior proof of the ATP’s inaccuracy of measuring the correct generated force. In terms of the actual testing, the inaccurate quantitative force results were used to discern whether one test provided higher lift force than another, but the values represented do not claim to be the absolute force measurements of the tested specimens.

To prove the lift force dependence of the flapping frequency, the mechanical flapper was secured to the ATP with one wing and the flapping frequency was increased from 15Hz to 25Hz. The expectation was that the lift force would increase with the flapping frequency, and thus the measured force from the ATP would also increase. The results of this test are shown in Figure \ref{fig:MechanicalFlapping}, where the force output of one wing is graphed as a function of time while the flapping frequency is increased. 
\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=8.5cm]{figures/MechanicalTest.png}
    \caption{Mechanical flapping test with one wing and increasing frequency from 15Hz to 25Hz}
    \label{fig:MechanicalFlapping}  
\end{figure}
The trend shown in the figure is a clear increase in the measured force as the flapping frequency increases, from about 0.03N at 15Hz start to about 0.05N at 25Hz. The "noise" seen in the figure is not a result of poor acquisition, but rather a flaw in the mechanical actuator design. The wing fastening mechanism was prone to slippage and thus the range of motion would change slightly with each flapping cycle, resulting in a different force output. While not an accurate enough measurement to quantify, it is a good example of how the ATP can be used to qualitatively analyze the performance of a tested mechanism and make design decisions based on that analysis.

The EM actuator was also tested with the same methodology, however discrete changes in frequency were made to better identify a singular condition. The results of this test are shown in Figure \ref{fig:EM_FQ}, where the force output of one wing is graphed as a function of time while the flapping frequency is changed. 
\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=8.5cm]{figures/EM_4F_Drift.png}
    \caption{EM actuator test with a single wing and different freuencies of 6, 8, 12, and 11 Hz}
    \label{fig:EM_FQ}  
\end{figure}
Each spike represents the condition where the EM actuator is flapping at a different frequency; 6, 8, 12, and 11 Hz in this example. While the results follow a constant drift throughout the test, as seen by the orange trendline, the rise in force due to the actuation can still be used to compare across each frequency. Tests were conducted in attempts to understand the drift; however different tests resulted in different directions of drift from the captured baseline. More interestingly, the amount of drift seen in this figure was only experienced when testing with the EM actuator and the flapping bird and mechanical actuator both returned within 0.01N of the start baseline. Unfortunately, further investigation was not conducted to resolve the issue as regardless of the drift, the qualitative analysis could still be conducted. The figure identifies the largest force output at 11Hz (the last peak), where the lowest force output is from the 12Hz. This is consistent with the range limitation of the EM actuator previously indicated by the EM actuator team, where it significantly reduces as a function of increased frequency after 11Hz. The results of this test qualitatively prove this as a function of force output instead of a purely visual representation. 

\subsection{Conclusion and Future Work}
The ATP has been developed this year to a point where it can be used as a qualitative analysis tool for a MFWF actuator. The analysis of the test results acquired should validate its functionality for measuring force from a specific actuator or wing design. However, there are many improvements that can be made to make it a more accurate and precise tool for measuring averaged forces from an actuator. The first improvement should be to determine the cause of baseline drift found most prominently within the EM actuator test. With validation of why this occurs, prevention methods can be used to more accurately identify the measured force. Another important improvement should be the implementation of a noise filter within the hardware. A measured force signal without high frequency noise will allow better signal averaging within software and a better representation of force. The largest change that should be made requires collaboration with the physical design changes of the ATP. The current accuracy limitation of the load strings is a result of load sharing within the inner frame and can be eliminated with a proper redesign. It is highly recommended that a FBD is conducted before manufacturing to verify the expected load path. In terms of electronics, the tension experienced on the load cells when implementing a redesign should be monitored as it will affect the required gain to achieve high resolution.

In terms of measuring the precise forces from the MFWF in a decoupled manner, the design of the ATP would have to be significantly changed. In terms of the electronics, SM S-type load cells are ideal for tension-compression measurements but limit the design to accommodate that. The AD8421 amplifiers are ideal for low noise applications but have internal limitations that require the design to be built around them. If a redesign were to happen with the goal of precise force measurement, it is recommended to spend the time to cherry pick the components required to achieve the best performance and critique their integration within the structural design.
